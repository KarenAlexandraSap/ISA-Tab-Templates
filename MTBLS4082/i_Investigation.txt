Study Identifier	MTBLS4082
Study Title	Angiopoietin-like 4 governs diurnal lipoprotein lipase activity in brown adipose tissue.
Study Description	<p><strong>OBJECTIVE: </strong>Brown adipose tissue (BAT) burns fatty acids (FAs) to produce heat, and shows diurnal oscillation in glucose and triglyceride (TG)-derived FA-uptake, peaking around wakening. Here we aimed to gain insight in the diurnal regulation of metabolic BAT activity.</p><p><strong>METHODS: </strong>RNA-sequencing, chromatin immunoprecipitation (ChIP)-sequencing and lipidomics analyses were performed on BAT samples of wild type C57BL/6J mice collected at 3-h intervals throughout the day. Knockout and overexpression models were used to study causal relationships in diurnal lipid handling by BAT.</p><p><strong>RESULTS: </strong>We identified pronounced enrichment of oscillating genes involved in extracellular lipolysis in BAT, accompanied by oscillations of FA and monoacylglycerol content. This coincided with peak lipoprotein lipase (Lpl) expression, and was predicted to be driven by peroxisome proliferator-activated receptor gamma (PPARγ) activity. ChIP-sequencing for PPARγ confirmed oscillation in binding of PPARγ to Lpl. Of the known LPL-modulators, angiopoietin-like 4 (Angptl4) showed the largest diurnal amplitude opposite to Lpl, and both Angptl4 knockout and overexpression attenuated oscillations of LPL activity and TG-derived FA-uptake by BAT.</p><p><strong>CONCLUSIONS: </strong>Our findings highlight involvement of PPARγ and a crucial role of ANGPTL4 in mediating the diurnal oscillation of TG-derived FA-uptake by BAT, and imply that time of day is essential when targeting LPL activity in BAT to improve metabolic health.</p>

Study Protocol Description	<p>All mice were housed under standard conditions with a 12 h:12 h light:dark schedule at 22 °C with <em>ad libitum</em> access to a chow diet (Rat and Mouse No.3 Breeding, SDS, Horley, United Kingdom) and water. All mouse experiments were conducted in accordance with the Institute for Laboratory Animal Research Guide for the Care and Use of Laboratory Animals and were approved by the National Committee for Animal experiments.</p><p><br></p><p>Non-fasted <strong>male C57BL/6J mice</strong> (Charles River Laboratories, Wilmington, MA, USA; 10 wk old) were killed by CO2 inhalation at <strong>8 time points</strong> over a 24-h period (corresponding to ZT0, ZT3, ZT6, ZT9, ZT12, ZT15, ZT18 and ZT21; n=4 per time point). Interscapular brown adipose tissue (<strong>iBAT</strong>) was collected and snap-frozen.</p>	<p>Lipids were extracted as previously described<strong>[1][2]</strong>. Briefly, BAT was homogenized in ultra-pure ice-cold water using the TissueLyser system (Qiagen, Hilden, Germany). Subsequently, the homogenates were sonicated in an ice-cold water bath for 10&nbsp;min. Protein concentrations were determined using the BCA protein assay kit (ThermoFisher Scientific, Waltham, MA, USA). Lipids were extracted using a single-phase extraction. In brief, 1.5&nbsp;mL of <strong>chloroform/methanol (1:1, v/v)</strong> and an <strong>internal standards mixture</strong> specifically adjusted to BAT (details below) was added to the tissue homogenate (the equivalent of 1&nbsp;mg protein). Subsequently, the mixture was sonicated in a water bath for 5&nbsp;min, followed by centrifugation at 4 °C (16,000 x g for 5&nbsp;min). The liquid phase was transferred to a glass vial and evaporated under a stream of nitrogen at 60 °C. Subsequently, the residue was dissolved in 150 µL of <strong>chloroform/methanol</strong> (9:1, v/v).</p><p><br></p><p>The following amounts of <strong>internal standards</strong> dissolved in 1:1 (v/v) <strong>chloroform/methanol</strong> were added to each sample: bis(monoacylglycero)phosphate BMP (14:0) 2, (0.2&nbsp;nmol); ceramide-1-phosphate C1P (d18:1/12:0), (0.127&nbsp;nmol); D7-cholesteryl ester CE(16:0), (2&nbsp;nmol);&nbsp;ceramide&nbsp;Cer(d18:1/12:0), (0.118&nbsp;nmol); ceramide Cer(d18:1/25:0), (0.130&nbsp;nmol);&nbsp;cardiolipin&nbsp;CL(14:0) 4, (0.1&nbsp;nmol);&nbsp;diacylglycerol&nbsp;DAG(14:0) 2, (0.5&nbsp;nmol); glucose ceramide GlcCer (d18:1/12:0), (0.126&nbsp;nmol); lactose ceramide LacCer (d18:1/12:0), (0.129&nbsp;nmol); lysophosphatidicacid LPA(14:0), (0.1&nbsp;nmol);&nbsp;lysophosphatidylcholine&nbsp;LPC (14:0), (0.5&nbsp;nmol);&nbsp;lysophosphatidylethanolamine&nbsp;LPE (14:0), (0.1&nbsp;nmol); lysophosphatidylglycerol LPG(14:0), (0.02&nbsp;nmol);&nbsp;phosphatidic acid&nbsp;PA (14:0) 2, (0.5&nbsp;nmol);&nbsp;phosphatidylcholine&nbsp;PC (14:0) 2, (2&nbsp;nmol);&nbsp;phosphatidylethanolamine&nbsp;PE (14:0)2, (0.5&nbsp;nmol);&nbsp;phosphatidylglycerol&nbsp;PG (14:0) 2, (0.1&nbsp;nmol);&nbsp;phosphatidylinositol&nbsp;PI (8:0) 2, (0.5&nbsp;nmol); phosphatidylserine&nbsp;PS (14:0) 2, (5&nbsp;nmol); sphinganine&nbsp;1-phosphate S1P (d17:0), (0.124&nbsp;nmol); sphinganine-1-phosphate S1P (d17:1), (0.125&nbsp;nmol); ceramide&nbsp;phosphocholines&nbsp;SM (d18:1/12:0), (2.129&nbsp;nmol); sphingosine&nbsp;SPH (d17:0), (0.125&nbsp;nmol); sphingosine SPH (d17:1), (0.125&nbsp;nmol); triacylglycerol TAG (14:0)2, (0.5&nbsp;nmol).&nbsp;</p><p><br></p><p><strong>Refs:</strong></p><p><strong>[1] </strong>Held, N.M., Buijink, M.R., Elfrink, H.L., Kooijman, S., Janssens, G.E., Luyf, A.C., Pras-Raves, M.L., Vaz, F.M., Michel, S., Houtkooper, R.H. and van Weeghel, M., 2021. Aging selectively dampens oscillation of lipid abundance in white and brown adipose tissue.&nbsp;<em>Scientific reports</em>,&nbsp;<em>11</em>(1), p.5932. doi:10.1038/s41598-021-85455-4. PMID:&nbsp;33723320.</p><p><strong>[2] </strong>Held, N.M., Kuipers, E.N., van Weeghel, M., van Klinken, J.B., Denis, S.W., Lombès, M., Wanders, R.J., Vaz, F.M., Rensen, P.C., Verhoeven, A.J. and Boon, M.R., 2018. Pyruvate dehydrogenase complex plays a central role in brown adipocyte energy expenditure and fuel utilization during short-term beta-adrenergic activation.&nbsp;<em>Scientific reports</em>,&nbsp;<em>8</em>(1), p.9562. doi:10.1038/s41598-018-27875-3. PMID:&nbsp;29934543</p>	<p>Lipids were analyzed using a <strong>Thermo Scientific Ultimate 3000 HPLC</strong>. For <span style='font-style: normal; color: rgb(54, 54, 54); font-weight: 700;'>reversed phase </span>separation, 2 μL of each sample was injected onto a <span style='font-style: normal; color: rgb(54, 54, 54); font-weight: 700;'>ACQUITY UPLC HSS T3 (1.8 µm, 2.1 mm x 150 mm; Waters)</span> column, with temperature at 60°C. Mobile phase consisted of (A) 4:6 (v/v) methanol:water and B 1:9 (v/v) methanol:isopropanol, both containing 0.1% formic acid and 10 mmol/L ammonia. Using a flow rate of 0.4 mL/min, the LC gradient consisted of: 0 min, 100% A; 1 min, 80% A; 16 min, 0% A; 16-20 min, 0% A; 20.1 min, 100% A; 20.1-21 min, 100% A.</p><p><br></p><p>For <strong style='font-style: inherit;'>normal phase </strong>separation, 2 μL of each sample were injected onto a <strong style='font-style: inherit;'>Luna silica (5 µm, 2 mm x 250 mm, 100Å; Phenomenex) </strong>column, with temperature at 25 °C. Mobile phase consisted of (A) 85:15 (v/v) methanol:water containing 0.0125% formic acid and 3.35 mmol/L ammonia and (B) 97:3 (v/v) chloroform:methanol containing 0.0125% formic acid. Using a flow rate of 0.3 mL/min, the LC gradient consisted of: 0-1 min, 10% A; 4 min, 20% A; 12 min, 85% A; 12.1 min, 100% A; 12.1-14 min, 100% A; 14.1 min, 10% A; 14.1-15 min, 10% A.</p><p><br></p>	<p>MS data were acquired with a <strong>Q Exactive Plus Orbitrap mass spectrometer</strong> using<strong> </strong>negative and positive ionization using continuous scanning over the range of <strong>150 to 2000 </strong><strong style='color: rgb(54, 54, 54);'>m/z</strong><span style='color: rgb(54, 54, 54);'>.</span></p>	<p>Data were analyzed using an in-house developed metabolomics pipeline written in the <a href='http://ww.r-project.org' rel='noopener noreferrer' target='_blank'><strong>R</strong></a> programming language. All reported lipids were normalized to corresponding internal standards according to lipid class.</p>	<p>Lipid identification has been based on a combination of accurate mass, (relative) retention times, fragmentation spectra and the injection of relevant standards.</p>
